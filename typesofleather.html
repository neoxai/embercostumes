Leather

Types of Leather

Veg-tan (Vegetable Tanned) is the most versatile and useful leather. Its the only leather that can be tooled and dyed easily. Most of our leather tutorials will be referring to Veg-tan leather unless otherwise noted.

Suede is leftover when veg-tan is split to make it more even. It doesn't have the tooling grain like the top of veg-tan, so both sides are "flesh". It cannot be tooled, and almost always comes pre-dyed. It tends to be thin and very flexible.

Chrome-tan is the expensive version of suede. It's what leather jackets are made of. I haven't had the desire to use it yet.

Upholstery leather is another expensive version of suede. If you can get it from an old couch, awesome. Otherwise ignore it.

Vinyl is not leather. It is often referred to as fake/faux-leather and such. It may look like leather from far away, and is a fraction of the cost, but it behaves more like plastic than leather. Great substitute for large or non-detailed items, but as a it doesn't breathe. It can be way hot and uncomfortable, and not good for outdoors in summer. On a budget, vinyl rocks. Temporary, short term costumes, vinyl is great. If you are going for smaller, higher quality pieces, veg-tan is what you need.



Thickness

Leather is measured in ounces. Since one piece is not a uniform thickness throughout, it will be listed as two numbers describing the range of thicknesses it can encompass. One "ounce" equals 1/64 of an inch.

2/3 oz: Accessories, linings, laces, soft compressible pieces.
4/5 oz: Pouches, small straps, embellishments.
6/7 oz: *Most Used* Belts, costume armor, straps and fittings.
8/9 oz: Combat armor, thick belts, reinforcements, cuir bouilli.



Animals

Most leather you will get from cows. For the most part, don't worry about anything else. Here's the quick run down of other animals leather can come from.

Deer have very thin, relatively tough skin. Worth experimenting with, it will drape more like fabric than cowhide.
Pigskin is one of the toughest leathers easily available. It makes a great liner for bags or sheaths, or as a durable piece all by itself.
Rabbit fur is very soft, warm and fragile. It can be used to line winter armor or even sewn to garments to increase their warmth.
Exotic skins such as snake, ray, or shark have one thing in common: expense. If used sparingly, they can add very interesting patterns and textures to inlays, sword hilts, and accessories.
